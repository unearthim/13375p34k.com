HISTORY OF ASCII ART


							
ASCII art emerged in the 1960s, when computers could only display text and users relied on letters, numbers, and symbols to create pictures. This creative workaround gained popularity in the 1970s and 80s, especially on Bulletin Board Systems (BBS), where talented “elite” users crafted banners, logos, and signature blocks using the limited ASCII character set.


							
As computers became social spaces, ASCII art intertwined with hacker and internet culture. Users swapped letters for numbers, inventing “leetspeak” (“31337” for “elite”), as a way to bypass filters and signal in-group status. By the 1990s, ASCII graphics and leet phrases dominated forums, IRC, and early websites, while wordplay like “n00b,” “pwn,” and “h4x0r” spread into memes and digital language.


							
Although modern computers now support graphics, ASCII art endures for its nostalgia and creative freedom. It’s still found in code comments, game chats, and internet memes—a testament to the ingenuity and humor of early digital communities.
